My Spiel

I am interested in understanding the behavior of humans, both as individuals and as groups, through computational methods and large-scale datasets that have only recently emerged due of their size and complexity. My current work falls into two areas. The first explores prosocial norms and altruistic decision-making through the lens of computer science. The second uses data ingest and visualization techniques to create tools to understand aggregate social phenomenon, such as journalistic trends. For both of these areas, I am also very interested in mapping the findings to industry and society for the aim of making the world a better place. I am currently pursuing my BA studying mathematics and computer science at Pomona College, in Claremont, California and am affiliated with the Human Cooperation Lab at Yale University.

Human Cooperation Lab

The lab brings together computer scientists, biologists, mathematicians, philosophers and of course, psychologists, to study prosociality: Why are people willing to help each other, and how can prosociality be promoted? The lab, directed by Dave Rand, uses a range of interdisciplinary methods to approach these questions, from economic games and online experiments to machine learning and computational modeling. My work in the lab included machine learning in economic games, computational game theory, Real world altruism, free will and moral judgement, an open source webcam and natural language processing.

Research in the News

Check out the press on Dave Rand's and my PLOS ONE paper, where we look at extreme altruism in the real world using testimonials from Carnegie Heroes. The paper was listed as one of the Top 10 Insights from the Science of a Meaningful Life in 2014 by the Greater Good Science Center at UC Berkeley and was featured in Vox, Slate, Science Daily, Reddit, New York Magazine and Medical Daily
